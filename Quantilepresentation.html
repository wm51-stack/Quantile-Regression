Demographic and Socioeconomic Predictors of Algebra 1 End-of-Course Achievement in Florida School Districts

Wendy Matende, Madison Edwards, Jegg Ononiewu

The Gateway: Algebra 1 EOC

  • The Gateway Course: Algebra 1 shapes student access to advanced mathematics, college readiness, and future STEM pathways.
  • High Stakes in Florida:
  • Must pass the End-of-Course (EOC) assessment to earn a standard high school diploma.
  • Accounts for 30% of a student’s final course grade.
  • Systemic Impact: Directly influences school accountability, remediation funding, and instructional support.

District-Level Disparities

  • Florida’s 67 school districts vary dramatically in demographic and socioeconomic composition.
  • Key Contextual Predictors:
  • Demographics: Racial/ethnic distributions (White, Black, Hispanic).
  • Socioeconomics: Poverty, unemployment, median household income, and bachelor’s degree attainment.
  • The Equity Problem: Resource-dense districts provide structural advantages, while economically disadvantaged communities face systematic barriers.

The Methodological Gap: Beyond the Mean

“One-size-fits-all statistical models can mask meaningful differences among lower-, middle-, and higher-performing groups.” — Reeves & Lowe (2009)

  • The Problem with OLS: Traditional mean-based regression assumes that demographic and socioeconomic impacts are uniform across all districts.
  • The Reality: Contextual influences vary. Factors affecting lower-achieving districts may operate differently in high-achieving ones (Tian, 2006; Haile & Nguyen, 2008).
  • The Florida Gap: Existing state studies (e.g., Holley, 2017) rely heavily on mean-based methods, leaving a critical gap in distribution-sensitive analysis.

The Solution: Quantile Regression

OLS Regression

  • Estimates the conditional mean.
  • Assumes constant predictor effects.
  • Can obscure variation in the tails of the distribution.

Quantile Regression

  • Estimates relationships at multiple points (\(\tau = 0.25, 0.50, 0.75\)).
  • Uncovers shifting magnitudes or changing signs across performance tiers.

Research Questions

  1. How are district-level demographic characteristics associated with Algebra 1 EOC achievement?
  2. How are district-level socioeconomic characteristics associated with Algebra 1 EOC achievement?
  3. Do the direction, magnitude, and significance of these predictors vary across the achievement distribution?
  4. Which predictors demonstrate the strongest associations at specific quantiles, and what are the implications for equity-focused planning?

Study Significance & Contributions

  • For Policymakers: Shifts focus from statewide averages to targeted resource allocation for struggling vs. excelling performance tiers.
  • For Educators: Deepens the understanding of how community-level structural barriers uniquely pressure mathematics achievement.
  • For Researchers: Fills a documented literature gap by applying a distribution-sensitive lens to Florida’s K–12 educational outcomes.

Methods Overview

Methods

  • Research Design: Quantitative, cross-sectional study
  • Analytical Method: Quantile Regression (QR)
  • Comparison Model: Ordinary Least Squares (OLS)
  • Software: R
  • Significance Level: \(\alpha = 0.05\)

Why Quantile Regression?

  • Examines relationships across the entire achievement distribution.
  • Reveals how predictors differ for lower-, middle-, and higher-performing districts.
  • Provides more information than OLS, which estimates only the average effect.

Key References

  • Koenker and Bassett (1978) | Reeves and Lowe (2009) | Costanzo and Desimoni (2017)

Quantile Regression Model

Model Specification

For each quantile, \(\tau\), the conditional quantile function is specified as:

\[ \begin{aligned} Q_\tau(Y|X) &= \beta_0(\tau) + \beta_1(\tau)\text{pct\_white} + \beta_2(\tau)\text{pct\_black} + \beta_3(\tau)\text{pct\_hispanic} \\ &\quad + \beta_4(\tau)\text{median\_income} + \beta_5(\tau)\text{poverty\_rate} \\ &\quad + \beta_6(\tau)\text{unemployment\_rate} + \beta_7(\tau)\text{bachelors\_rate}. \end{aligned} \]

Variable Definitions

  • \(Y\): District-level mean Algebra 1 EOC scale score.
  • \(X\): Vector of demographic and socioeconomic predictors.
  • \(\beta(\tau)\): Quantile-specific regression coefficients.
  • \(\tau\): The conditional quantile being estimated.

Quantiles Estimated

  • 10th, 25th, 50th, 75th, 90th

QR allows predictor effects to vary across different levels of district achievement (Koenker & Ng, 2005; Davino et al., 2013).

  • ■ Conducted all analyses using R.

Results and Discussion

Purpose of the Analysis

This section presents the findings in the order the analyses were conducted.

  • Descriptive analysis
  • Relationships between socioeconomic variables and achievement
  • OLS regression
  • Quantile regression
  • Policy implications

Distribution of Algebra 1 Mean Scores

Key Findings & Interpretation

  • Clustering: Mean scores clustered tightly between ~390 and 405.
  • Low Variation: District variation across Florida was relatively small.
  • Meaningful Gaps: Despite a compressed range, small variations reflect key socioeconomic inequalities (Reardon et al. (2019), Sirin (2005)).

Answer to Research Question 1:

✔ Baseline district achievement varies within a narrow but meaningful distribution.

Poverty and Algebra 1 Achievement

Key Findings & Interpretation

  • Negative Relationship: Higher-poverty districts generally exhibited lower mean scores.
  • Confounded Effect: Overlaps with income and education; loses significance in multivariate OLS models.
  • Structural Variable: Reflects broad structural disadvantage rather than operating independently (Sirin (2005)).

Answer to Research Question 2:

✔ Poverty is negatively associated with achievement, but lacks an independent multivariate effect.

Median Household Income

Key Findings & Interpretation

  • Positive Bivariate Trend: Higher-income districts show structurally better performance trends (Duncan and Murnane (2016)).
  • Multicollinerality: Insignificant in OLS due to a high correlation with community educational attainment levels.

Answer to RQ 2:

✔ Median household income shows a positive bivariate relationship but not an independent multivariate effect.

Bachelor’s Degree Attainment

Key Findings & Interpretation

  • Strong Predictor: Robust positive association across the entire distribution.
  • Strongest Impact: Effect intensifies at higher-performing districts.
  • Literature Match: Confirms parental and community educational capital drives student success (Reardon et al. (2019)).

Answer to Research Question 2:

✔ Bachelor’s degree attainment was one of the strongest predictors of achievement.

Unemployment and Achievement

Key Findings & Interpretation

  • Consistent Harm: Higher unemployment tracks lower achievement.
  • Remains Significant: Holds strong and negative in multivariate OLS and across all quantiles.
  • Context: Economic instability directly impacts localized student learning environments (Duncan and Murnane (2016)). Answer to Research Question 2:

✔ Unemployment was a strong and consistent negative predictor.

Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) Results

Predictor Breakdown

  • Significant Predictors:

  • 👍 Bachelor’s Degree Attainment (Positive impact)

  • 👎 Unemployment Rate (Negative impact)

  • Non-Significant Predictors:

  • Poverty, Median Income, % White, % Black, and % Hispanic (primarily due to overlapping explanatory variances).

Interpretation & Limitations

  • Average Relationships: OLS evaluates global, average effects across your entire dataset exclusively.
  • Uniformity Bias: It assumes identical effects across all districts, masking crucial differences at the top and bottom tails (Koenker and Bassett (1978)).

Answer to Research Question 3: ✔ OLS successfully described average trends, but completely missed unique shifts across different school achievement levels.

Why Quantile Regression?

Beyond the Mean

  • OLS Limitation: OLS only estimates relationships at the conditional mean.
  • Quantile Advantage: Quantile regression models relationships at specific, distinct points across the entire distribution.

Achievement Levels Modeled (\(\tau\))

  • \(\tau = 0.10\) & \(0.25\): Low-Achieving Districts
  • \(\tau = 0.50\): Median-Achieving Districts
  • \(\tau = 0.75\) & \(0.90\): High-Achieving Districts

Analytical Advantages

  1. Context-Dependent Dynamics: Identifies if predictors vary based on whether a district is low-, middle-, or high-performing.
  2. Unmasks Hidden Patterns: Reveals critical structural patterns that are hidden completely by standard OLS averages.

Lowest-Achieving Districts (\(\tau = 0.10\))

Main Findings

  • Demographic Sensitivity: Race-related variables showed their strongest negative effects at this tail:

  • pct_black: -0.88331

  • pct_white: -0.83163

  • pct_hispanic: -0.81958

  • Economic Instability: Unemployment acts as a highly severe negative predictor at this specific level.

Interpretation & Insights

  • Structural Disadvantage: Low-performing school districts experience the most intense compounding influence from systemic disadvantages.
  • Compounded Inequalities: Racial disparities at this level are heavily connected with structural and environmental inequality factors (Owens (2017)).

Answer to Research Questions 3 & 4:

  • Key Takeaway: Predictor effects are strongest among the lowest-performing districts; demographic makeup and localized unemployment heavily dominate performance outcomes at the bottom tail.

Middle Achievement Districts (\(\tau = 0.25\) & \(\tau = 0.50\))

Main Findings

  • Demographic Fade: Race-related variable coefficients weaken substantially compared to the lowest tail.
  • Rise of Education Capital: The impact of Bachelor’s degree attainment rates increases.
  • Persistent Stress: Unemployment continues to exert a sturdy negative pull on results.

Interpretation & Insights

  • Shifting Dynamics: As districts pull closer toward state averages, structural-demographic vulnerabilities diminish slightly.
  • Community Capital: Local educational assets emerge as stronger, more dominant engines of student performance (Reardon et al. (2019)).

Answer to Research Questions 3 & 4: ✔ Predictor effects change across the distribution, with educational capital becoming increasingly influential in middle-performing systems.

Highest Achievement Districts (\(\tau = 0.75\) & \(\tau = 0.90\))

Main Findings

  • Dominant Asset: Bachelor’s degree attainment reaches its peak effect size (0.47533 at \(\tau = 0.90\)).
  • Demographics Neutered: Race and demographic composition become completely non-significant at this tail.
  • Constant Harm: Unemployment remains a steady, unyielding negative factor even in these top-tier systems.

Interpretation & Insights

  • Educational Concentration: High-performing districts are primarily distinguished and sustained by their localized concentration of adult educational capital.
  • Advantage Accumulation: This pattern strongly reinforces an “accumulation of advantage” framework across top-performing student cohorts (Bryk (2015)).

Answer to Research Questions 3 & 4: ✔ Educational attainment completely dominates among high-achieving districts, while demographic composition drops entirely in predictive power.

Correlation Matrix

Key Findings & Core Logic

  • Positives: Test scores track heavily with Income (r = 0.72) and Bachelor’s degrees (r = 0.65).
  • Negatives: Poverty and Income share a severe inverse relationship (r = -0.82).
  • Collinearity: Income vs. Bachelor’s degree rates are profoundly linked (r = 0.82).

Interpretation & Insights

  • Systemic Interconnection: Socioeconomic predictors are deeply interconnected, rather than operating in isolation (Sirin (2005)).
  • OLS Dropouts: This severe multicollinearity directly explains why powerful individual variables drop out of statistical significance when thrown together into a multi-variable OLS model.

Answer to Research Questions 1 & 2: ✔ District achievement scores are molded by clusters of interconnected context, not single isolated variables.

Quantile Regression Coefficient Plot

Main Dynamic Patterns

  1. Unemployment: Consistently flat and negative across every single quantile layer.
  2. Bachelor’s Degrees: Increases steadily, escalating from lowest to highest quantiles.
  3. Demographics: Exhibits its strongest drag at low quantiles, then levels off at higher quantiles.

Interpretation & Insights

  • Visual Proof: This visual trend confirms the absolute existence of strong heterogeneous effects across the achievement distribution, which traditional mean-based OLS completely hides (Koenker and Bassett (1978)).

Answer to Research Question 3: ✔ Quantile regression successfully revealed heterogeneous effects that were completely ignored or missed by standard OLS.

Overall Quantile Regression Findings

Summary of Distributional Realities

  • At the Bottom (\(\tau = 0.10\)): Demographic variables and systemic marginalization matter most to performance outcomes.
  • At the Top (\(\tau = 0.90\)): Regional adult educational capital dominates and drives exceptional achievement.
  • Across the Entire Board: Localized unemployment acts as a consistent, unyielding negative barrier to student success.

Core Presentation Takeaway

  • Beyond Global Averages: Educational inequality cannot be treated as a single, uniform number.
  • Multi-Layered Reality: Structural barriers change their behavior depending on whether a school system is fighting to catch up or working to maintain its advantage.

Research Questions Takeaway: ✔ Research Questions 3 and 4 were answered comprehensively by demonstrating that predictor effects vary sharply across the achievement distribution. Educational inequality is structural, non-uniform, and deeply multi-layered.

Policy Implications

Finding Policy Implication
High Unemployment Expand tutoring, mental health, and attendance support
Low Educational Attainment Invest in community education and workforce development
Low-Achieving Districts Provide targeted interventions and wraparound services
Different Effects Across Quantiles Avoid one-size-fits-all educational policies. School interventions must adjust based on performance distribution layers (Bryk (2015))

Contribution, Limitations, and Future Research

Contributions

  • Applied quantile regression to Florida Algebra 1 performance data.
  • Discovered heterogeneous effects missed by classic OLS.
  • Advanced contextual educational inequality literature.

Limitations

  • Cross-sectional data (no causal pathways).
  • District-level aggregation (susceptible to ecological fallacy).
  • Lacks school-level variables (funding details, teacher experience).

Future Work

  • Longitudinal tracking.
  • Multilevel modeling structures (students nested inside districts).
  • Cross-state comparisons to test model generalizability.

Conclusion

Core Presentation Takeaways

  • Socioeconomic Primacy: Localized socioeconomic profiles heavily dictate Algebra 1 outcomes across Florida.
  • The Stability Tax: Unemployment consistently acts as an achievement depressor across all distribution percentiles.
  • Methodological Value: Quantile regression surfaces critical, hidden policy insights that mean-based OLS models completely wipe out.

🎯 Final Take-Home Message

The Core Reality: Educational inequality is not uniform across Florida school districts.

  • Distributional Insights: Quantile regression provides a far more comprehensive and nuanced understanding of how socioeconomic and demographic factors influence achievement.
  • Tailored Policy: By looking beyond the average, we can see exactly how these factors change their behavior at different student performance levels—allowing for more precise, targeted policy interventions.
